# Li Lu (李录)'s Major Career and Investment Decisions: An Empirical Research Report

This research report documents the critical career transitions and investment decisions of Li Lu (李录), founder of Himalaya Capital (喜马拉雅资本), detailing the context, execution, and outcomes of these choices. 

---

## 1. Career Decisions

### 1.1 The 1993 Warren Buffett Lecture: The Shift to Value Investing
*   **The Decision:** Shifting from skepticism about the stock market to a lifetime commitment to value investing.
*   **Context & Execution:** In 1993, while studying at Columbia University, Li Lu attended a guest lecture by Warren Buffett. Li Lu has recounted that he attended by accident, having mistaken "Buffett" for "Buffet" (free buffet lunch) due to his nascent English language skills. Buffett explained the core principles of value investing: viewing stocks as fractional ownership of a business, the margin of safety, and market fluctuations as "Mr. Market" offering opportunities rather than dictating value.
*   **Significance:** Prior to the lecture, Li Lu viewed the stock market as a speculative, zero-sum casino. Buffett’s lecture convinced him that investing could be a win-win partnership with high-quality businesses. This single event altered his career path from law/politics to professional investing.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Li Lu, Lecture at Peking University Guanghua School of Management (2014), published in *Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing and China* (《文明、现代化、价值投资与中国》).
    *   *Secondary Source:* Wikipedia: Li Lu; *The Investors Podcast* (2023).

### 1.2 Founding Himalaya Capital (1997) & Shifting from Shorting/VC to Long-Only
*   **The Decision:** Founding Himalaya Capital in late 1997 and subsequently transitioning the fund's strategy away from short-selling and venture capital toward a pure, long-only public equity value investing model.
*   **Context & Execution:** 
    *   Li Lu founded Himalaya Capital in late 1997 during the height of the Asian Financial Crisis. In its first year (1998), the fund suffered a 19% decline, and a major early backer withdrew their capital. 
    *   In the late 1990s, the fund was structured to manage both a venture capital pool and a public hedge fund. Li Lu experimented with short-selling, day trading, and arbitrage to survive.
    *   Ultimately, Li Lu abandoned shorting, citing the fact that shorting has "unlimited downside risk" and exposes the investor to high psychological strain that is incompatible with a long-term compounder mindset. He also wound down active venture capital operations to focus solely on deep-value, concentrated public equity investing.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Li Lu, *Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing and China* (2020); Himalaya Capital Official Bio/History (`himcap.com`).
    *   *Secondary Source:* Wikipedia: Himalaya Capital; *Sophialand Finance* research reports (2024).

### 1.3 Declining the Berkshire Hathaway Portfolio Manager Offer (2010)
*   **The Decision:** Withdrawing himself from consideration for a chief investment officer/portfolio manager position at Berkshire Hathaway.
*   **Context & Execution:**
    *   In July 2010, *The Wall Street Journal* published a front-page article indicating that Li Lu was a top contender to manage a significant portion of Berkshire Hathaway’s $100 portfolio. Charlie Munger was quoted stating that Li Lu's hiring was a "foregone conclusion."
    *   Later in 2010, Berkshire announced the hiring of Todd Combs, and it was revealed that Li Lu had voluntarily withdrawn his name from consideration.
    *   **Contradictions/Varying Interpretations:** 
        *   *Independence View:* The primary cited reason was Li Lu's desire to preserve his professional independence. Managing his own boutique firm, Himalaya Capital, gave him complete autonomy over his concentrated holdings without the institutional constraints, public scrutiny, and corporate reporting required at Berkshire.
        *   *Political/Background View (Secondary Speculation):* Several financial commentators suggested that Li Lu's status as a former prominent student leader of the 1989 Tiananmen Square protests—who was placed on the Chinese government's "21 Most Wanted" list—would have created significant political friction and diplomatic complications for Berkshire Hathaway in China (especially given Berkshire's massive stake in BYD and interest in Chinese markets). Neither Buffett, Munger, nor Li Lu have ever publicly confirmed this political angle.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Warren Buffett, Comments at Berkshire Hathaway Annual Meeting/Press Interviews (2011); Charlie Munger, Daily Journal Annual Meeting Transcript (2011).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *The Wall Street Journal* (July 2010 reporting); *The Guardian* (October 2010 reporting on Todd Combs' hiring and Li Lu's withdrawal); *Forbes* (2010 succession speculation).

## 2. Major Investment Decisions

### 2.1 Concentrated Investment in BYD (2002/2008)
*   **The Decision:** Building a massive concentrated stake in BYD starting in 2002, and pitching the company to Charlie Munger, which led to Berkshire's landmark 2008 investment.
*   **Context & Execution:**
    *   **2002 Initial Position:** Li Lu first purchased BYD shares shortly after its Hong Kong IPO in 2002. At the time, BYD was a small mobile phone battery manufacturer. Li Lu was deeply impressed by founder Wang Chuanfu's engineering mindset, frugality, and ability to manufacture high-quality batteries at a fraction of the cost of multinational competitors. Himalaya Capital acquired roughly 1.67% of BYD's total equity.
    *   **The Munger Connection (2008):** In 2003, Li Lu met Charlie Munger. By 2004, Munger entrusted Li Lu with $88 million of his family's personal wealth (the only outside manager Munger ever gave money to). Li Lu concentrated a significant portion of Munger's funds into BYD. In 2008, Li Lu introduced BYD to Munger, describing Wang Chuanfu as "a combination of Thomas Edison and Jack Welch." This convinced Berkshire Hathaway to buy a 10% stake (225 million H-shares) for $230 million in September 2008.
*   **Outcome:** The BYD investment became one of the most profitable investments in Berkshire history, growing more than 30-fold at its peak.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Charlie Munger, Interviews/Daily Journal Annual Meetings (multiple years); HKEX Disclosure of Interests (Himalaya Capital / BYD).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *Business Insider* (2023 profile of Munger/Li Lu relationship); *Dave Lu Blog* (archived Munger/Li Lu details).

### 2.2 Kweichow Moutai (贵州茅台)
*   **The Decision:** Acquiring a large position in Kweichow Moutai when it traded at historically low valuation multiples.
*   **Context & Execution:** 
    *   Li Lu initiated the Moutai investment in the early-to-mid 2000s. According to Charlie Munger, Li Lu identified Moutai's massive economic moat (a national brand with virtual monopoly status in premium Chinese *baijiu* and significant pricing power) and bought shares when the stock was trading at just **four to five times earnings** (P/E 4-5x). Munger remarked that Li Lu "backed up the truck" and "made a killing" on Moutai.
    *   *Unrealized Berkshire Deal:* Chinese investment manager Dan Bin (但斌) later revealed that he accompanied Li Lu to visit Moutai's facilities multiple times as Li Lu was researching the company on behalf of Warren Buffett/Berkshire. However, a direct investment by Berkshire never materialized due to disagreements on terms or valuation, though Li Lu invested via Himalaya Capital.
*   **Outcome:** The position became a "287-bagger" over its holding period, significantly boosting Himalaya Capital's early returns.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Charlie Munger, Comments at Daily Journal Annual Meetings; Dan Bin (微博/公开访谈 on Moutai visits with Li Lu).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *Financial Times* (profile on Li Lu); *Shipra Capital* Case Study on Moutai.

### 2.3 Postal Savings Bank of China (PSBC / 邮储银行)
*   **The Decision:** Building a major stake in a large Chinese state-owned retail bank in late 2020 and early 2021.
*   **Context & Execution:** 
    *   In December 2020 and January 2021, Himalaya Capital purchased over 1 billion H-shares of Postal Savings Bank of China, crossing the 5% disclosable threshold on the HKEX. The investment was estimated at HK$4.3 billion (approx. USD $550 million).
    *   *Investment Thesis:* PSBC possessed a unique retail network (utilizing China Post's massive rural and urban network), an extremely low non-performing loan (NPL) ratio compared to other major Chinese state banks, and traded at a deep discount to book value (P/B ratio under 0.6x).
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Hong Kong Exchanges and Clearing (HKEX) Disclosure of Interests filings (December 2020 - January 2021).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *Futu News* (富途资讯) / *U-Smart HK* regulatory tracking reports.

### 2.4 Portfolio Evolution: US Tech Stocks (Alphabet, Micron, Pinduoduo)
*   **The Decision:** Concentrating Himalaya Capital's U.S. portfolio (13F holdings) in major technology firms, specifically Alphabet (Google), Pinduoduo (PDD Holdings), and a historical swing trade in Micron Technology.
*   **Context & Execution:**
    *   **Alphabet (Google):** Alphabet represents the largest holding in Himalaya’s U.S.-listed portfolio, comprising approximately 44.8% of reported 13F holdings as of Q1 2026.
    *   **Micron Technology (MU):** Li Lu built a massive position in Micron starting in Q4 2019, accumulating over 11 million shares. In Q1 2023, the fund reduced its stake by ~40.5%, and completely exited the position in Q2 2023 (selling the remaining 6.82 million shares). While the investment was profitable (estimated ~27% lifetime return), the exit occurred just before the stock skyrocketed due to the AI-driven High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) boom.
    *   **Pinduoduo (PDD):** Built into a major holding, representing approximately 14.7% of the 13F portfolio by Q1 2026.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* SEC Form 13F Filings for Himalaya Capital Management LLC (2019–2026).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *HedgeFollow*, *GuruFocus*, and *Holdings Channel* database aggregations.

## 3. Behavior vs. Statement: Alignments & Contradictions

### 3.1 The 1989 Activist vs. Pragmatic Capitalist (The Ideological Transition)
*   **The Tension:** Reconciling his past as a radical democracy activist on China's "Most Wanted" list with his current position as a pragmatist investing in Chinese state-linked firms and promoting China's economic system.
*   **Alignment/Explanation (His Perspective):** In his book *Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing and China*, Li Lu explains his worldview through the lens of "Modernization 3.0." He argues that modernization is driven by the integration of technology and a free-market economy. Under this framework, China's economic rise is a natural result of its adoption of market mechanisms. He has embraced pragmatism (inspired by Lee Kuan Yew), advocating for governance models that replicate what works rather than adhering to rigid ideological dogmas. 
*   **Contradiction/Controversy (Critic's Perspective):** Critics and human rights observers have noted the irony of a former student leader, who fled political persecution, later praising the efficiency of the Chinese economic model and investing heavily in state-controlled banks (like Postal Savings Bank of China) and state-linked conglomerates. His return to China in 2010 alongside Buffett and Gates—under strict government media guidelines that forbade local press from photographing or mentioning him—highlighted this unresolved tension between his political past and financial present.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Li Lu, *Civilization, Modernization, Value Investing and China* (2020); Radio Free Asia (RFA) / Voice of America (VOA) reporting on the 2010 China trip restrictions.
    *   *Secondary Source:* *Medium* analytical articles (2023); *Observer.com* (early 2000s profiles on Li Lu's transition to Wall Street).

### 3.2 "Buy and Hold" vs. The 2021 BYD Trim
*   **The Tension:** Championing ultra-long-term investing ("holding a business forever if it remains great") while executing major profit-taking sales.
*   **Alignment/Explanation (Value Investing Perspective):** In July 2021, Himalaya Capital sold 10.8 million H-shares of BYD for HK$2.44 billion, reducing its stake from 7.03% to roughly 6%. Value investors defend this as standard portfolio rebalancing. Following a 500% surge in BYD's stock price during 2020, the position had grown to dominate the fund's overall asset allocation, violating risk parameters. 
*   **Contradiction/Controversy:** The trade triggered temporary market panic. Because Li Lu had spent two decades lecturing on the compounding power of never selling outstanding businesses, his reduction of BYD shares was viewed by some retail investors as a sign of bearishness or a contradiction of his stated philosophy, though Himalaya Capital still maintained a multi-billion dollar stake in the company.
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* HKEX Shareholding Disclosures (July 2021).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *South China Morning Post* (SCMP) / *Bloomberg* coverage of the July 2021 transaction.

### 3.3 Condemning Short-Selling after Practical Failure
*   **The Tension:** Li Lu frequently states that value investors should never short stocks, yet he actively shorted stocks in his early career.
*   **Alignment/Explanation:** Li Lu has openly reconciled this by acknowledging his early shorting activities as a critical mistake. He states that he shorted companies to keep Himalaya Capital afloat during the 1997-1998 crisis, but the experience proved that shorting is structurally flawed (as losses are theoretically infinite while gains are capped at 100%) and psychologically destructive. 
*   **Citations:**
    *   *Primary Source:* Li Lu, Lectures on Value Investing at Columbia Business School (2006) and Peking University (2014).
    *   *Secondary Source:* *ValueSider* / *Rational Walk* articles compiling his investing rules.
